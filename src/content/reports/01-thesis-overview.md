## Position

Canada has a clear policy commitment to electrification, a deep federal and provincial funding stack, and a meaningful execution gap. It also has a defensive trade posture toward Chinese-made vehicles. The right entry vector is not the vehicle.

## The wedge

Charging infrastructure, battery storage, O&M software, and home charging are simultaneously:

1. **Underserved** relative to the 100% ZEV-by-2035 trajectory.
2. **Eligible for cost-share** under federal and provincial programs.
3. **Less politically loaded** than vehicle imports, especially when delivered through a Canadian-incorporated entity with local partners.

This combination produces a capital-efficient validation path — the company can be in market, generating revenue, and earning regulatory credibility long before a vehicle decision needs to be made.

## What we are not doing in year one

- Direct import of finished Chinese-made EVs at scale.
- Greenfield local assembly without a validated commercial base.
- Public-facing branding that obscures ownership or origin.

## What we are doing

- Standing up a Canadian-incorporated operating entity.
- Deploying commercial DCFC and fleet depot charging in two priority provinces.
- Pairing chargers with BESS to manage interconnect risk.
- Operating an OCPP 2.0.1 / ISO 15118 software platform hosted in a Canadian cloud region.
- Building a recurring O&M and software business that can serve third-party site hosts.

## Why this matters for the long-term option

The infrastructure-first entry is not a substitute for a future vehicle play — it is the option that makes a future vehicle play credible. Operators that have already built sites, employed Canadians, paid Canadian tax, and partnered with Canadian utilities will be evaluated very differently if and when the policy environment evolves.
