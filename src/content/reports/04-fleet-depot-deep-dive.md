## Why fleets

Fleets compress most of the variables that make public DCFC hard: utilization is contracted, locations are known, and customers care about uptime, not branding. Depot operators are also the most likely to value the full stack — chargers, BESS, telematics integration, and managed-charging software — as a single bundle.

## Partner archetypes

- **Municipal transit agencies** — long lead time, large eventual size, often constrained by procurement rules. Worth a multi-year engagement but not a year-one revenue bet.
- **Last-mile logistics** — fastest moving, BESS-friendly, software-receptive. Likely the first depot pilot.
- **Regional utility own-fleet** — dual benefit: revenue plus a co-development relationship with the utility itself.
- **Mid-size logistics and food distribution** — strong fit; depots already exist and have power infrastructure that can be augmented.

## BESS-buffered depot design

A typical depot pilot might combine 4–10 DCFC dispensers, a megawatt-class BESS, and managed-charging software that staggers vehicle charging across an overnight window. The BESS:

- Caps peak grid draw, reducing demand charges.
- Defers or eliminates utility upgrade costs.
- Provides backup and resilience.
- Can participate in capacity or ancillary markets where regulation permits.

## Commercial structure

Two viable models:

1. **Charging-as-a-service (CaaS)** — operator owns the depot equipment and bills per kWh / per session against a multi-year contract.
2. **Build-own-operate hand-off** — operator builds and operates for 3–5 years, then sells the asset to the fleet or to a yieldco; software and O&M contract continues.

Both can be capitalized with a mix of equity, ZEVIP cost-share, and CIB long-term debt.
