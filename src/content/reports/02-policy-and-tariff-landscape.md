## Federal posture

The federal government has set a 100% zero-emission new-vehicle sales target for 2035, with interim targets including 20% by 2026, codified through the Electric Vehicle Availability Standard. Charging deployment is funded primarily through ZEVIP (cost-share grants) and the Canada Infrastructure Bank's ZEV Charging Initiative (long-term debt).

Trade posture has tightened. Canada has applied a 100% surtax on Chinese-made electric vehicles since 2024, and additional duties on certain steel and aluminum products. Procurement processes for critical infrastructure increasingly include national-security and supply-chain reviews.

**Implication:** A vehicle-led entry is structurally exposed. An infrastructure, storage, and software-led entry is navigable but must be executed through a Canadian entity with transparent ownership, Canadian data residency, and Canadian partners.

## Provincial differentiation

- **Ontario** — Largest fleet base, active Save On Energy / EV ChargeON programs. Long interconnect queues are a real constraint.
- **Québec** — Highest EV adoption per capita, integrated utility partner in Hydro-Québec, strong residential L2 channel.
- **British Columbia** — CleanBC and BC Hydro both active in managed charging and depot electrification. Mountain corridor charging is undersupplied.
- **Alberta** — Fleet and resource sector opportunities, but provincial policy is less aligned with federal targets.
- **Atlantic provinces** — Smaller but cohesive markets with provincial utilities; useful for branded corridor coverage.

## What this means for sequencing

Lead with Ontario and Québec for the first 18 months. Use BC as the second wave. Treat Alberta and the Atlantic provinces as opportunistic until the base is proven.
